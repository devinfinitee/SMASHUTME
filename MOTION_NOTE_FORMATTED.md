SECTION: Types of Motion

DEFINITION: Motion refers to a continuous change in the position of a body relative to a fixed reference point. The main types are linear (straight line), circular (curved path), oscillatory (back and forth), and random (no fixed path).

JAMB POINT: JAMB frequently asks candidates to identify types of motion from given scenarios. Know that a pendulum swings in oscillatory motion, a car on a straight road moves in linear motion, and electrons around a nucleus move in circular motion.

EXPLANATION: Linear motion occurs when a body moves in a straight line — either uniform (constant speed) or non-uniform (changing speed). Circular motion occurs when a body moves along a circular path at constant speed but changing velocity (because direction keeps changing). Oscillatory motion is repetitive back-and-forth movement about a fixed point. Random motion has no definite path — molecules in a gas exhibit random motion.

EXAMPLES:
- A ball thrown horizontally off a cliff is projectile motion (combination of linear motions)
- A ceiling fan blade spinning shows circular motion
- A child on a swing experiences oscillatory motion
- Smoke particles rising demonstrate random motion

QUICK TIP: Even though a body in circular motion moves at constant speed, its velocity is not constant because velocity is a vector and its direction keeps changing. JAMB loves this distinction. Remember: speed constant = possible; velocity constant = impossible in circular motion.

AI EXPLANATION: Think of motion types like the ways you can move around your compound. Walking straight to the gate is linear. Running around a circular flower bed is circular motion. Swinging on a rope tied to a mango tree is oscillatory. A fly buzzing around with no pattern is random. The key thing JAMB tests is whether you can look at a real-life scenario and correctly name what type of motion is happening.

SECTION: Distance, Displacement, Speed and Velocity

DEFINITION: Distance is the total length of path covered by a moving body regardless of direction — it is a scalar quantity. Displacement is the shortest straight-line distance between the starting point and ending point in a specified direction — it is a vector quantity. Speed is the rate of change of distance. Velocity is the rate of change of displacement.

JAMB POINT: JAMB regularly tests the difference between distance and displacement using circular or zigzag path problems. If a body completes one full circle, its distance equals the circumference but its displacement is zero because it returns to the starting point. This is a guaranteed trick question.

EXPLANATION: Speed = Distance ÷ Time (scalar — no direction). Velocity = Displacement ÷ Time (vector — has direction). Average speed = Total distance ÷ Total time. Average velocity = Total displacement ÷ Total time. A body can have constant speed but changing velocity — this happens in circular motion. A body can have zero velocity but non-zero speed only instantaneously (at the turning point of oscillation).

EXAMPLES:
- A student walks 3m East then 4m North. Distance = 3 + 4 = 7m. Displacement = √(3² + 4²) = √25 = 5m (North-East direction)
- A car travels around a circular track of radius 7m and returns to start. Distance = 2πr = 44m. Displacement = 0m

QUICK TIP: Whenever you see a question involving a body that returns to its starting point — displacement is always zero regardless of the path taken. This is a guaranteed JAMB trick question. Also note: speed is always positive, but velocity can be negative if direction is opposite to the chosen reference direction.

AI EXPLANATION: Here is the simplest way to understand this. Imagine you are at your hostel and you walk to the market (500m away) and then walk back. You have covered a distance of 1000m — that is every step you took. But your displacement is zero because you ended up exactly where you started. Distance counts every step. Displacement only cares about where you started and where you finished, in a straight line. That is why a runner on a 400m track has covered 400m distance but zero displacement when they return to the start line.

SECTION: Equations of Motion (Kinematics)

DEFINITION: The equations of motion are mathematical relationships connecting displacement (s), initial velocity (u), final velocity (v), acceleration (a), and time (t) for a body under uniform acceleration.

JAMB POINT: This is the single most tested subtopic in Motion. Expect at least two to three direct calculation questions per paper. Master all four equations and know which variables are missing so you can select the right equation instantly under time pressure.

EXPLANATION: The four equations of motion under uniform acceleration are: Equation 1: v = u + at (use when displacement is not involved). Equation 2: s = ut + ½at² (use when final velocity is not given). Equation 3: v² = u² + 2as (use when time is not given). Equation 4: s = ½(u + v)t (use when acceleration is not given). For a body thrown vertically upward: At maximum height, v = 0. Acceleration = -g = -10 m/s² (deceleration going up). Coming down: acceleration = +g = +10 m/s². Time to reach maximum height = Time to come back down (symmetry).

EXAMPLES:
- A car starts from rest and accelerates uniformly at 4 m/s² for 5 seconds. u = 0, a = 4 m/s², t = 5s. s = ut + ½at² = 0(5) + ½(4)(25) = 50m
- A ball is thrown upward with velocity 20 m/s. Find maximum height. v = 0 at max height, u = 20 m/s, a = -10 m/s². v² = u² + 2as → 0 = 400 + 2(-10)s → s = 20m
- A body moving at 30 m/s decelerates at 5 m/s². How long before it stops? v = 0, u = 30, a = -5. v = u + at → 0 = 30 + (-5)t → t = 6 seconds

QUICK TIP: Write out what you know and what you need to find before touching any equation. List u, v, a, s, t and mark which ones are given. The missing variable tells you exactly which equation to use. This saves precious seconds in JAMB CBT. Remember: SUVAT — S, U, V, A, T are the five variables.

AI EXPLANATION: These four equations are your motion calculator. Every JAMB motion calculation problem gives you three things and asks you to find a fourth. Your job is to identify what you have and what is missing, then pick the equation that connects those exact variables. Start from rest means u equals zero. Comes to rest means v equals zero. Moving up means acceleration is negative ten (negative g). Moving down means acceleration is positive ten. Once you memorise those four trigger phrases, half the problem is already solved before you pick up your calculator. For example, if a question gives u, a, t and asks for s, only equation 2 contains all four — so you use s = ut + ½at².

SECTION: Motion Graphs

DEFINITION: Motion graphs are graphical representations of how distance, displacement, speed, or velocity of a body changes with time. The two most important are the distance-time graph and the velocity-time graph.

JAMB POINT: JAMB tests graph interpretation heavily — particularly calculating acceleration from a velocity-time graph (slope) and distance from the area under a velocity-time graph. These are guaranteed marks if you know the rules. Expect at least one graph question per paper.

EXPLANATION: Distance-Time Graph: Horizontal line = body is stationary (not moving). Straight diagonal line = uniform speed (constant velocity). Curved line (getting steeper) = acceleration. Curved line (getting less steep) = deceleration. Slope of distance-time graph = speed. Velocity-Time Graph: Horizontal line = constant velocity (zero acceleration). Straight line sloping up = uniform acceleration. Straight line sloping down = uniform deceleration. Slope of velocity-time graph = acceleration. Area under velocity-time graph = distance (displacement). Calculating Area Under Graph: Rectangle area = length × width. Triangle area = ½ × base × height. Trapezium area = ½(a + b) × h.

EXAMPLES:
- Reading a v-t graph: A body accelerates from 0 to 20 m/s in 4 seconds, maintains 20 m/s for 6 seconds, then decelerates to rest in 2 seconds. Acceleration phase: a = (20-0)/4 = 5 m/s². Total distance = area under graph. Triangle 1: ½ × 4 × 20 = 40m. Rectangle: 6 × 20 = 120m. Triangle 2: ½ × 2 × 20 = 20m. Total = 180m

QUICK TIP: In a velocity-time graph, a straight line going up that starts from zero always forms a triangle with the time axis. The area of that triangle is the distance covered during acceleration. Never forget: area = distance, slope = acceleration. Also note that a horizontal line on a velocity-time graph means zero acceleration (constant velocity), not zero velocity unless the line is at zero.

AI EXPLANATION: Think of a velocity-time graph like a receipt for a journey. The slope tells you how fast the speed was changing — that is your acceleration. The area under the line tells you how far the body actually travelled — that is your distance. A flat horizontal line means cruise control — constant speed, zero acceleration. A steep upward line means the body is picking up speed very quickly. A line going back down to zero means the body is braking. Every shape the graph makes — triangle, rectangle, trapezium — is a distance waiting to be calculated. For a distance-time graph, the slope tells you speed — flat means stopped, straight sloping means constant speed, curving means changing speed.

SECTION: Projectile Motion

DEFINITION: Projectile motion is the motion of a body launched into the air at an angle to the horizontal, moving under the influence of gravity alone. The horizontal and vertical components of motion are completely independent of each other.

JAMB POINT: JAMB tests the independence of horizontal and vertical motion, time of flight, maximum height, and range. A body thrown horizontally from a height and a body dropped from the same height hit the ground at the same time — this principle appears regularly. Also note that range is maximum at 45°.

EXPLANATION: Key Principle: Horizontal velocity remains constant throughout (no horizontal acceleration). Vertical motion is affected by gravity (a = g = 10 m/s²). For a projectile launched at angle θ with initial velocity u: Horizontal component: uₓ = u cosθ. Vertical component: uᵧ = u sinθ. Time of flight: T = 2u sinθ / g. Maximum height: H = u² sin²θ / 2g. Range: R = u² sin2θ / g. Maximum range occurs at θ = 45° (sin90° = 1). For a body projected horizontally from height h: Time to reach ground: t = √(2h/g). Horizontal distance: x = uₓ × t. Vertical velocity on impact: vᵧ = gt.

EXAMPLES:
- A ball is projected at 30° with velocity 40 m/s. Find the range. (g = 10 m/s²). R = u² sin2θ / g = (1600 × sin60°) / 10 = (1600 × 0.866) / 10 = 138.6m
- A stone is thrown horizontally at 15 m/s from a cliff 20m high. How far from the base does it land? t = √(2h/g) = √(40/10) = √4 = 2 seconds. x = 15 × 2 = 30m from base

QUICK TIP: The range formula R = u² sin2θ / g means that 30° and 60° give the same range (because sin60° = sin120° = same value). JAMB uses this pair regularly. Also remember range is maximum at 45° — sin90° = 1 which gives the highest possible value. When a question says "projected horizontally", it means angle = 0°, so uₓ = u and uᵧ = 0.

AI EXPLANATION: Projectile motion is simply two separate motions happening at the same time and you solve them separately. Horizontal is easy — nothing pushes the ball sideways so it moves at constant speed the whole time. Vertical is just free fall — gravity pulls it down at 10 m/s² the whole time. The ball goes forward because of its horizontal speed and falls down because of gravity. These two motions combine to create the curved path you see. The trick in every JAMB projectile question is to split the motion into horizontal and vertical, solve each one separately, then combine only when the question asks you to. For example, to find how far it lands, you need time from vertical motion, then multiply by horizontal speed.

SECTION: Newton's Laws of Motion

DEFINITION: Newton's three laws of motion describe the relationship between force and the motion of a body.

JAMB POINT: All three laws are tested. Newton's first law appears in questions about inertia. Newton's second law appears in F = ma calculations. Newton's third law appears in questions about action-reaction pairs, rockets, and recoil. Know that action and reaction act on DIFFERENT bodies — this is the most common trap.

EXPLANATION: First Law (Law of Inertia): A body remains in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by an external force. Inertia is the resistance of a body to change in its state of motion. Larger mass = greater inertia. Second Law: The rate of change of momentum of a body is directly proportional to the applied force and takes place in the direction of the force. F = ma (force = mass × acceleration). F = (mv - mu) / t (force = change in momentum / time). Unit of force: Newton (N) = kg m/s². Third Law: For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. Forces always act in pairs. Action and reaction act on different bodies. They are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.

EXAMPLES:
- First Law: A passenger lurches forward when a bus brakes suddenly — the passenger's body wants to continue moving forward (inertia) while the bus stops
- Second Law: A 5kg object accelerates at 3 m/s². Force = 5 × 3 = 15N
- Third Law: A rocket expels gas downward (action) and the rocket moves upward (reaction). A gun recoils backward when a bullet fires forward

QUICK TIP: For Third Law questions, always identify the two bodies involved. Action and reaction never act on the same body. A common JAMB trap is listing forces on the same body as action-reaction pairs — they are not. Example: weight of a book on a table and normal force from table are NOT action-reaction pairs (they act on the same body).

AI EXPLANATION: Newton's first law is basically saying objects are lazy — they do not want to change what they are doing unless something forces them to. That is why a stationary chair stays still until you push it, and why you slide forward in a car when it stops suddenly — your body wants to keep moving. Newton's second law gives you the maths — more force means more acceleration, more mass means less acceleration for the same force. Newton's third law is the push-back law — whenever you push something it pushes back on you with exactly the same force. That is why a gun kicks back when you fire it, why a rocket flies upward by pushing gas downward, and why you move backwards slightly when you jump off a boat onto a dock.

SECTION: Momentum and Impulse

DEFINITION: Momentum is the product of the mass of a body and its velocity. It is a vector quantity measured in kg m/s. Impulse is the product of force and the time for which it acts, equal to the change in momentum of a body.

JAMB POINT: The law of conservation of momentum is a major JAMB topic — particularly for collision problems. Know the difference between elastic collisions (kinetic energy conserved) and inelastic collisions (kinetic energy not conserved). JAMB also tests impulse calculations directly. When bodies stick together, it is perfectly inelastic.

EXPLANATION: Momentum: p = mv. Impulse: J = Ft = mv - mu = Δp. Conservation of Momentum: Total momentum before collision = Total momentum after collision. m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂. Elastic Collision: Both momentum AND kinetic energy are conserved. Bodies bounce off each other. Perfectly Inelastic Collision: Momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is NOT fully conserved. Bodies stick together after collision. m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = (m₁ + m₂)v.

EXAMPLES:
- A 4kg ball moving at 6 m/s collides with a stationary 2kg ball and they stick together. Find their common velocity. 4(6) + 2(0) = (4+2)v. 24 = 6v. v = 4 m/s
- A force of 10N acts on a 2kg body for 3 seconds starting from rest. Find the final velocity. Impulse = Ft = 10 × 3 = 30 Ns. 30 = mv - mu = 2v - 0. v = 15 m/s

QUICK TIP: When two bodies stick together after collision it is always a perfectly inelastic collision. Use the combined mass on the right side of the equation. This is the most common collision type JAMB tests. For elastic collisions, both momentum and kinetic energy equations are needed to solve for final velocities.

AI EXPLANATION: Momentum is basically how hard something would be to stop. A heavy truck moving slowly and a small bullet moving very fast can have the same momentum — mass times velocity. The conservation law says that in any collision the total momentum before equals the total momentum after, as long as no outside force is acting. Think of it like money in a transaction — what goes in must come out, just distributed differently between the two bodies. Impulse is what changes momentum — a large force for a short time (like a hammer hitting a nail) or a small force for a long time (like pushing a car) can produce the same change in momentum. The formula Ft = mv - mu means force multiplied by time equals the change in momentum.

SECTION: Circular Motion

DEFINITION: Circular motion is the motion of a body along a circular path. Even at constant speed, the body is constantly accelerating because its direction continuously changes. This acceleration always points toward the centre of the circle and is called centripetal acceleration.

JAMB POINT: JAMB tests centripetal force calculations, the relationship between period, frequency and speed, and why a body in circular motion is said to be accelerating even at constant speed. Banking of roads and satellite motion are common application questions. Remember: centrifugal force is NOT a real force — it is the sensation of being pushed outward due to inertia.

EXPLANATION: Key Formulae: Angular velocity: ω = 2π/T = 2πf. Linear velocity: v = rω = 2πr/T. Centripetal acceleration: a = v²/r = rω². Centripetal force: F = mv²/r = mrω². Period: T = 1/f (f = frequency in hertz). Important Concepts: Centripetal force is not a new force — it is the name given to whatever force is providing the inward pull (tension, gravity, friction, normal reaction depending on context). For a car on a flat road: friction provides centripetal force. For a satellite orbiting Earth: gravity provides centripetal force. For a ball on a string: tension provides centripetal force.

EXAMPLES:
- A body of mass 2kg moves in a circle of radius 3m at 4 m/s. Find the centripetal force. F = mv²/r = 2 × 16 / 3 = 10.67N
- A wheel rotates at 5 revolutions per second. Find the angular velocity. f = 5 Hz, ω = 2πf = 2π × 5 = 10π = 31.4 rad/s

QUICK TIP: Centripetal means centre-seeking. The force always points inward toward the centre. There is no outward centrifugal force in reality — what you feel pushing you outward in a turning car is your inertia resisting the change in direction, not an actual outward force. JAMB occasionally tests whether centrifugal force is real — it is not a real force. It is sometimes called a fictitious force.

AI EXPLANATION: Here is the key insight about circular motion — speed and velocity are different things. Speed is just how fast you are going. Velocity includes direction. A body going around a circle keeps the same speed but its direction changes every instant, which means its velocity keeps changing, which means it is always accelerating. That acceleration points inward toward the centre — centripetal means centre-seeking. Whatever is providing that inward pull is the centripetal force. For a ball on a string it is tension. For a planet orbiting the sun it is gravity. The formula F equals mv squared over r tells you how much inward force is needed to keep the body moving in that circle. If that inward force disappears (string breaks, friction gone), the body flies off in a straight line — not outward, but tangent to the circle.
